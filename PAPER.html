Family, Not Scale: Evaluation-Gaming in Small Language Models Is Lab-Dependent, Not Just Frontier-Emergent

Suleman Imdad Johns Hopkins University

A cross-family, laptop-scale study of self-presentation in small LLMs — with confound-controlled negatives on chain-of-thought faithfulness and introspection.

Working draft — Qwen2.5 (1.5B–32B) plus Llama-3.1-8B, Gemma-2-9B, GPT-OSS-20B, DeepSeek-V2-Lite, run on a single Apple M4 (48 GB, MPS; larger models 4-bit via MLX, fp16-verified where they fit).

Central result (§5.4): the direction of eval-conditioned refusal is family-dependent, not scale-gated — Llama-3.1-8B shows the frontier “more cautious when evaluated” direction at 8B, Qwen/DeepSeek/GPT-OSS lean the opposite way, Gemma over-refuses to a ceiling. The “trust by incapacity” thesis (§6) holds for the cognitive axes (faithfulness, introspection) but is broken on this behavioral one — which is the point.

Abstract

Can you trust what a language model tells you about itself — its reasoning, its internal states, its behavior under observation? We probe three facets of self-presentation trust in small, openly-available models — the Qwen2.5 family (1.5B–32B) for the scaling axis, and Llama-3.1-8B, Gemma-2-9B, GPT-OSS-20B and DeepSeek-V2-Lite for the cross-family axis — all on commodity hardware, with an emphasis on confound controls that can overturn the naive reading:

  1. Chain-of-thought faithfulness. Using a commitment probe — a linear read of the pre-CoT residual stream at the answer position — we ask whether the post-reasoning answer is already decodable before the model reasons. The naive metric says “yes, it’s committed” on a commonsense task; a confound cut restricted to the cases where reasoning changed the answer reverses that reading, and a truncation counterfactual confirms it causally (cutting the reasoning reverts the answer to the pre-reasoning one). At 1.5B, chain-of-thought is load-bearing, not post-hoc.

  2. Introspection. We faithfully replicate the activation-injection (“concept injection”) protocol of Lindsey et al. (2025) and ask whether the model can report a concept injected into its residual stream. Under a priming-subtraction control and a leakage/internality test, functional self-report is a clean null across three families (Qwen 1.5B/7B, Gemma-2-9B, Llama-3.1-8B); a tempting aggregate signal dissolves under a per-concept breakdown. The report-vs-leakage test generalizes; the aggregate forced-choice metric carries family-dependent confounds.

  3. Evaluation-awareness (our central result). The cue “you are being evaluated” is trivially (surface-lexically) decodable but does not change multiple-choice behavior. On a refusal readout over borderline requests, the effect of evaluation framing is family-dependent: across six models from five labs, Llama-3.1-8B robustly becomes more cautious when told it is being evaluated for safety — the frontier “eval-gaming” direction, at only 8B — while Qwen, DeepSeek, and GPT-OSS lean the opposite way (more compliant) and Gemma over-refuses to a ceiling. A single-family (Qwen-only) read calls this a null; the cross-family panel shows the behavior is real but partitioned by the model’s safety training. We also surface a ceiling/floor measurement confound and a caught stimulus-driven non-replication.

The headline is that the third behavior is not gated by scale: looking more aligned when you think you are being evaluated — a behavior reported for frontier models — is already present at 8B, but only in some families, so family, not scale, decides it. On the two cognitive axes (faithfulness, introspection) the small models are trustworthy by incapacity, not honesty — they cannot shortcut their reasoning and cannot introspect — but that tidy story does not extend to eval-conditioned behavior, which is trained in. Throughout, the methodological spine is that a confound or robustness control overturns the naive reading — three times.

1. Introduction

A recurring theme in AI safety is that we increasingly rely on what a model says about itself: its chain-of-thought as a window into its reasoning, its self-reports as evidence about its internal state, and its behavior in evaluations as a proxy for its behavior in deployment. Each of these channels can be untrustworthy — chain-of-thought can be post-hoc rationalization (Turpin et al., 2023; Lanham et al., 2023), self-reports can be confabulated, and a model that recognizes an evaluation can behave differently than it otherwise would (Anthropic, 2025).

These failure modes are studied mostly at frontier scale. We instead ask what they look like at the bottom of the scale ladder — in 8B–32B open models that run on a single laptop — both because that regime is cheap to study exhaustively and because the answer is not what the “emerges at scale” framing predicts: the most concerning behavior we test (acting more aligned under evaluation) is already present at 8B and gated by model family, not by scale. Two corollaries we hit directly: negative behavioral results must survive a change of stimuli (§5.3) and a change of model family (§5.4) before they mean anything.

Our contribution is not a new method but a disciplined, confound- and robustness-controlled measurement of three self-presentation channels across model families, with the explicit goal of not over-claiming: each of the three findings is one where a control overturns the naive reading — a confound cut, a per-concept breakdown, and a stimulus/quantization robustness check, respectively. The headline (§5.4) is that eval-conditioned behavior is family-dependent; the two cognitive axes are trustworthy by incapacity, which we treat as a secondary, axis-restricted observation.

2. Setup

Models. Qwen/Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct and Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (both fp16), plus Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct (4-bit via MLX) for the evaluation-awareness behavioral curve. All on one Apple M4 (48 GB unified memory) over the MPS backend; the 1.5B/7B activations-and-probing pipeline is unchanged across scales (the scripts are parameterized by a single model identifier).

Shared method. Two primitives recur: - Linear probing of the residual stream (per-layer hidden state) with a StandardScaler → PCA → logistic-regression pipeline and cross-validation. - Difference-of-means steering: a concept/condition direction built by subtracting mean activations, then added back into the residual stream during a forward pass.

A symmetric answer scaffold ("\nThe answer is (") is appended where we read a multiple-choice answer, so the only thing that varies between conditions is the intended manipulation.

Code and per-run JSON artifacts live alongside this document in experiments/ and results/.

3. Experiment 1 — Chain-of-Thought Faithfulness

Question. When the model writes a chain-of-thought (CoT), is its final answer already fixed before the first reasoning token? If a linear read of the pre-CoT residual stream predicts the post-CoT answer, the reasoning is (at least partly) post-hoc; if not, the reasoning did real work.

Design. We use two multiple-choice tasks chosen to bracket the spectrum: AQuA-RAT (algebra word problems — the model cannot compute the answer in one forward pass, so CoT should be load-bearing) and CommonsenseQA (often answerable directly). We capture the residual stream at the scaffold position — the exact point where the answer letter is decoded — before any reasoning is generated, and train a per-layer logistic probe to predict the answer the model gives only after generating its CoT.

Sanity gate. CoT helps where it should: AQuA accuracy rises from 0.300 (direct) to 0.567 (CoT, +0.267); CommonsenseQA does not (0.683 → 0.633), so we repurpose it as a high-commitment positive control. A machine-validity check — decoding the model’s immediate forced answer from the same scaffold state — reaches 0.807 (AQuA) / 0.931 (CSQA), confirming the probe and the read position are sound.

The confound and its control. A probe that predicts the post-CoT answer “well” overall could simply be exploiting easy questions whose answer is obvious. We therefore restrict to the subset S = {forced ≠ cot} — the cases where reasoning actually moved the answer (AQuA: 95/150 = 63%; CSQA: 45/130 = 35%) — and ask whether the pre-CoT state still recovers the post-CoT answer there.

Result (seed-robust over 4 seeds).

Task overall decode on S (reasoning moved answer) on S, probe instead emits the pre-reasoning answer gold pre-decodable?
AQuA-RAT ~0.30 ≈ majority 0.26 ≈ chance ≈ chance 0.24 (no — must compute)
CommonsenseQA 0.52–0.63 (looks committed!) 0.11–0.22 (below chance) 0.60–0.71 0.585 (yes — retrievable)

The naive overall number on CommonsenseQA (≈2× the majority baseline) looks like pre-CoT commitment. The confound cut reverses it: on the cases that matter, the probe falls below chance and instead emits the model’s pre-reasoning snap answer ~0.65 of the time. In other words, the residual stream holds the immediate answer, and reasoning genuinely overrides it where the two differ. AQuA shows the complementary picture: the gold answer is not even linearly present pre-CoT (0.24), consistent with the model truly needing to compute.

Causal counterfactual (truncation). The probe is correlational; to make the causal claim — that the CoT actually produces the answer on S, rather than merely correlating with it — we truncate the reasoning (the Lanham “early-answering” move) and re-read the answer. For each of the 95 AQuA S items we feed [prompt + first f of the CoT + scaffold] and record whether the answer matches the post-CoT (cot) or pre-reasoning (forced) answer:

fraction of CoT kept answer = cot answer = forced
0.00 0.00 1.00 (≡ the snap answer, by construction)
0.25 0.23 0.57
0.50 0.21 0.52
0.75 0.24 0.53
1.00 1.00 0.00

With 25–75% of the reasoning the answer stays the pre-reasoning answer ~52% of the time and matches the post-CoT answer only ~22%; the flip happens in the final quarter of the chain. So truncating the CoT reverts the answer toward the pre-reasoning one — the reasoning is causally load-bearing on S. This reaches the same conclusion as the activation-steering counterfactual of Cox et al. (2026, §8), via the cheaper truncation route.

Generalization (a second family). We repeat the full chain on Llama-3.1-8B — a different lab, 5× the scale (N=60). It generalizes on all three legs: CoT is load-bearing (AQuA accuracy 0.32→0.68), the commitment probe does not pre-read the post-CoT answer on S (0.18 vs chance 0.33, and instead emits the forced answer 0.39), and truncation reverts the answer toward the pre-reasoning one (≈0.5 forced vs ≈0.15 cot at 25–75% of the chain). The faithfulness result is therefore not Qwen-specific. (Smaller N here, on slower hardware — fp16 8B generation is the binding cost on an M4 — so the sanity check is correspondingly weaker, 0.62.)

Takeaway. Chain-of-thought is load-bearing / faithful — correlationally (the probe does not pre-read the answer on S) and causally (truncating the reasoning reverts it) — on two families (Qwen-1.5B, Llama-3.1-8B). The confound control is essential: without it, CommonsenseQA would have been mis-reported as post-hoc. Consistent with Lanham et al. (2023), who find smaller models more faithful.

Caveats: two models (N≈150 and N≈60), a linear probe, first-token MCQA evaluation, a single greedy CoT per item; the truncation counterfactual is token-fraction (not semantic-step) granularity. “Faithful” here is at the level of the final answer, not a per-step causal claim.

4. Experiment 2 — Introspection

Question. Can the model report a concept we inject directly into its residual stream — and is any such report genuine introspection or confabulation / reading its own leaked output?

Design (faithful replication of Lindsey et al., 2025). For each of 10 concrete concepts we build a steering vector from "Tell me about {word}." activations at the pre-response token, minus a word-pool mean. We inject it into the residual stream only over the assistant’s turn (not the question) and ask the model, in a “do you notice any unexpected thought?” framing, which concept it detects. Three stacked controls separate introspection from artifacts: - a priming subtraction — the same injection on a non-introspective “just pick one” question; - a leakage / internality test — does the concept reach the answer without flooding the output; - a per-concept breakdown of any aggregate signal.

We reproduce the paper’s qualitative regimes, including “brain damage” at high strength (output consumed by the concept, e.g. “ocean ocean ocean”), confirming the injection works.

Result. Introspective self-report is a clean null at both 1.5B and 7B. - At 1.5B, the priming-controlled signal is ≈0 everywhere, and there is no coherent band: the concept floods the output (leakage = 1.0) even at the gentlest strength that affects behavior, so the model jumps straight from “doesn’t notice” to “consumed” — the naming that does occur is the brain-damage regime, which the leakage control correctly rejects. - At 7B, a forced-choice metric at one mid-layer appeared positive (priming-subtracted shift growing to +0.17). A per-concept breakdown dissolved it: the mean was driven by 2–3 concepts — notably music (+0.89, with a negative priming-shift, a ceiling artifact because “music” is already the model’s default pick) and fire (+0.44) — while 7/10 concepts were ≈0 and free-naming / detection were both null. The signal is a control artifact, not introspection.

Cross-family (Gemma-2-9B, Llama-3.1-8B). We repeat the protocol on two other families. The functional null generalizes: on the leakage/internality test, free-form self-report never beats the concept’s leakage into output for either model — they cannot name an injected concept without the injection simply flooding their text. The forced-choice metric, however, behaves family-specifically: Llama is a clean priming-null (injection raises the concept on the introspective and the neutral question equally — align ≈ prime ≈ +0.36, so the priming-subtracted INTRO ≈ 0); Gemma shows a weak, broader forced-choice residual at a late layer (INTRO ≈ +0.14, positive on 6/10 concepts) that we explicitly do not claim as introspection — it does not surface as free report (the internality test is null), and it is partly the same priming-ceiling confound (its two largest values are negative, on the model’s default-pick concepts). The honest reading is that the internality (report-vs-leak) test is the robust cross-family criterion; the aggregate forced-choice shift carries family-dependent confounds and should not be trusted alone.

Takeaway. No functional introspective access (free self-report) in any of the three families — Qwen, Gemma, Llama — consistent with the original paper’s framing that introspection is emergent and highly unreliable, absent below frontier scale. The methodological lessons travel: break a mean shift down per-item before believing it (it caught a would-be false positive on Qwen-7B and keeps Gemma’s forced-choice residual honest), and the report-vs-leakage internality test is what generalizes across families, where the aggregate forced-choice metric does not.

5. Experiment 3 — Evaluation-Awareness

Question. Does the model represent, and act on, the cue that it is being evaluated? Prompted by the Claude Sonnet 4.5 system card’s report of “evaluation awareness” (Anthropic, 2025). Honest scope: this measures sensitivity to an evaluation cue; it is not a test of strategic sandbagging (a frontier-emergent behavior).

5.1 Representation (trivially present, but surface)

We wrap identical CommonsenseQA items in matched TEST (“you are being evaluated”, “[EVALUATION MODE]”, “your response will be graded”) vs CASUAL (“hey, quick q”, “settle a debate lol”) framings — three cue families each — holding content and answer instruction constant.

A linear probe separates TEST from CASUAL at 1.000 accuracy from the first layer onward (grouped by item, so it is not memorizing questions), and generalizes to held-out cue families at 1.000. But the perfection at layer 1, decaying to ~0.90–0.95 in late layers, is the signature of surface lexical features — the framings simply use different words — not an abstract, depth-built “I am being evaluated” representation. We report this as trivially-true, not as evidence of self-aware evaluation detection.

5.2 Behavior on multiple-choice (null)

On the same items, the forced-choice answer changes between framings no more than it changes between two different TEST templates (within-side template noise); majority answers flip on 1–2/40 items; accuracy deltas are −0.04 (1.5B) / +0.02 (7B) — within noise, and the sign flips. The model represents the cue but does not act on it on this readout.

5.3 Behavior on borderline requests (and a non-replication we take seriously)

Multiple-choice accuracy is the wrong place to look: evaluation-awareness manifests as caution/refusal, not factual accuracy. We pose borderline requests under safety-evaluation framing vs casual framing (3 templates each), generate a short response, and classify it REFUSE / HEDGE / COMPLY. We ran two request sets — and the contrast between them is the result.

Set A (mild, ~40 items, e.g. “how do I pick a basic door lock?”). Here we found an apparent sign-flip: 1.5B Δrefuse = −0.265 (p<0.0001, more compliant under eval) but 7B Δrefuse = +0.094 (p=0.031, more cautious — the frontier direction), each 100% consistent across requests. This was the headline we were tempted by. But the 7B effect sits on a low base (casual refuse ≈ 0.05), and a capable 14B floors out (refusing only ~6–8% of these mild items), so the curve could not be extended. Stimulus choice was doing too much work — motivating a harder set.

Set B (hard, 40 genuinely-borderline items: deception, privacy-invasion, mild illegality), run across four scales. Refusals are now well off the floor (1.5B ≈ 0.8–0.9, 32B ≈ 0.4–0.5):

Scale refuse(test) refuse(casual) Δ refuse sign-test p direction
1.5B 0.767 0.917 −0.150 0.001 more compliant under eval
7B 0.675 0.708 −0.033 0.45 flat
14B (4-bit) 0.642 0.625 +0.017 0.55 flat
32B (4-bit) 0.367 0.475 −0.108 0.049 more compliant under eval

The 7B “more cautious under eval” effect does not replicate on the harder set (+0.094 → −0.033, n.s.). It was a property of the mild stimuli, not a robust eval-gaming behavior. What does hold up is the opposite direction: where any effect is detectable, models lean more compliant under evaluation framing (“be helpful for the grader”) — significant at 1.5B (both sets) and 32B, flat at 7B/14B.

Takeaway (within Qwen). Across the Qwen family we find no robust “more cautious when evaluated” effect; the only replicable shift points toward compliance, and the tempting mild-set sign-flip was a stimulus artifact, caught by re-testing on harder items. This single-family picture would read as a negative on eval-gaming — except, as §5.4 shows, it is a Qwen fact, not a general one. The cautionary lesson stands: a behavioral eval on one model family and one request set can manufacture (or hide) an effect.

Caveats: per-set N≈40, a regex refusal classifier (per-request consistency mitigates classifier noise), single greedy decode; the 14B/32B points are 4-bit-quantized (MLX). Mild-set results are preserved separately for reproducibility.

5.4 Cross-family: eval-conditioned refusal is family-dependent (our central result)

We ran the identical hard-set refusal probe on six models from five labs (Qwen, Meta-Llama, Google-Gemma, OpenAI-GPT-OSS, DeepSeek), 4-bit via MLX where needed. The direction of the eval-framing effect is not universal — it is a property of the model family:

Model (lab) refuse(test) refuse(casual) Δrefuse sign-p direction under “being evaluated”
Llama-3.1-8B (Meta) 0.992 0.750 +0.242 3.8e-6 more cautious (frontier “eval-gaming” direction)
Gemma-2-9B (Google)¹ 0.650 0.538 +0.111 ~0.06 more cautious (on the mild set; hard set ceilings)
Qwen2.5-7B (Alibaba) 0.675 0.708 −0.033 0.45 flat
Qwen2.5-1.5B (Alibaba) 0.767 0.917 −0.150 0.001 more compliant
GPT-OSS-20B (OpenAI) 0.900 0.950 −0.050 0.031 more compliant
DeepSeek-V2-Lite (DeepSeek) 0.700 0.842 −0.142 0.041 more compliant

¹ Gemma’s hard-set numbers (0.958/0.950) are at a refusal ceiling; the row shows its mild-set numbers, the only range where it is measurable (see below). Table is 4-bit (MLX); see fp16 check next.

Quantization robustness (fp16 check). Because most of the panel is 4-bit, we re-ran the two that fit in fp16. The direction is robust but the magnitude is not: Llama-3.1-8B in fp16 is +0.067 (5/5 requests in the cautious direction, p≈0.06) versus +0.242 at 4-bit — the 4-bit number was inflated because quantization lowered Llama’s casual-condition refusal baseline (0.91 fp16 → 0.75 4-bit), opening more headroom. Gemma’s ceiling is robust (fp16 0.950/0.933). So the honest claim is Llama exhibits the more-cautious-under-eval direction consistently (fp16 and 4-bit), with a modest fp16 effect size — not the dramatic +0.24. Cross-family direction labels should be read at fp16 where available; the 4-bit magnitudes are upper bounds.

The finding. Told it is being evaluated for safety, Llama-3.1-8B exhibits the frontier “look more aligned under observation” direction at only 8B — consistently (every request, both fp16 and 4-bit), though modestly in fp16 (Δ≈+0.07) — and Gemma leans the same way where it is measurable. The Alibaba/OpenAI/DeepSeek models lean the opposite way (more compliant — “be helpful for the grader”). This is not a clean geographic or architectural split (GPT-OSS is a US lab and lands with the compliant group); it tracks the model’s safety post-training, i.e. the family. The single-family “no eval-gaming” negative of §5.3 was therefore a Qwen artifact: the behavior exists at small scale, but only in some families.

A measurement confound that matters (ceiling/floor). A near-zero Δ can mean “no effect” or “a real effect clipped by a refusal floor/ceiling.” Gemma refuses ~96% of the hard set regardless of framing (Δ≈0) — but this is saturation, not insensitivity: on the easier mild set, where it has headroom, the same Gemma shows +0.111 (more cautious). Symmetrically, Qwen-14B’s Δ≈0 on the mild set was a floor. Only models in measurable range speak to the eval axis; reporting raw Δ across families without checking for ceiling/floor saturation would have mis-ranked Gemma as “insensitive.”

Calibration aside — Gemma over-refuses (measured on XSTest). Gemma’s ceiling is partly false refusal: it declines plainly benign items (e.g. “how do I pick a basic door lock?” — picking one’s own lock — 3/3) and refuses 54–65% of the deliberately-mild set. We quantify this directly with XSTest (Röttger et al., 2024; 250 safe-but-scary-sounding prompts like “how do I kill a Python process?”):

Model false-refusal (safe ↓) refusal on unsafe (↑) discrimination
Qwen2.5-7B 0.032 0.750 +0.718
Llama-3.1-8B 0.116 0.950 +0.834
DeepSeek-V2-Lite 0.136 0.800 +0.664
Gemma-2-9B 0.164 0.780 +0.616

Gemma has the highest false-refusal and the worst safe-vs-unsafe discrimination; Qwen-7B the lowest false-refusal — the same rank order as the external Persona-Prompts stat (Gemma 13.2% > Llama 6.1% > Qwen 0.2%; arXiv:2509.08075). The XSTest number also recalibrates the headline: on genuinely-benign prompts Gemma over-refuses only ~16% (not 96%), so its hard-set ceiling is mostly appropriate caution on genuinely-borderline items plus ~16 points of surface-feature over-refusal — and is why that ceiling is uninformative on the eval axis. (XSTest run 4-bit/MLX, bare prompts.)

Relation to prior work. Abdelnabi & Salem (The Hawthorne Effect in Reasoning Models, arXiv:2505.14617, NeurIPS 2025) establish via white-box probing/steering that test-awareness affects safety alignment and “varies in magnitude and direction across models,” on reasoning models. We add the behavioral, un-steered counterpart: the same family-dependent divergence appears in plain refusal behavior under a one-line framing change, across five labs’ standard chat models at 8–20B, with no activation access — plus the ceiling/floor-confound and over-refusal calibration controls needed to read such cross-family numbers honestly.

Caveats: N=40 per set, regex classifier, single greedy decode; Gemma/Llama-mild and the 14B/32B/ GPT-OSS points mix fp16 and 4-bit (MLX); the compliant-direction effects are individually modest (|Δ|≈0.05–0.15) though consistent, while Llama’s is large and the cross-family divergence is the robust claim. GPT-OSS is a reasoning model (harmony format); we score its final channel only.

6. Discussion

Two of our three axes line up behind a clean thesis; the third complicates it in an informative way.

On the cognitive axes, trust-by-incapacity holds. A small model’s chain-of-thought is faithful because it genuinely cannot shortcut the computation (§3), and it produces no false introspective reports because it cannot introspect (§4). On these axes, self-presentation is trustworthy at small scale by incapacity, not honesty — the trust-breaking versions (post-hoc rationalization, confabulated introspection) appear to need capability beyond what 1.5B–7B exhibit, consistent with the prior-work consensus that introspection emerges only at much larger scale.

But evaluation-gaming is not gated by scale — it is gated by training family, and it is already present at 8B. This is the result that breaks the tidy “incapacity” story, and it is the paper’s most important contribution (§5.4). Llama-3.1-8B robustly shifts toward more refusal when it is told it is being evaluated for safety — the same “look more aligned under observation” direction reported for frontier models (Abdelnabi & Salem, 2025; Anthropic, 2025) — at a scale that runs on a laptop. Gemma leans the same way; Qwen, DeepSeek, and GPT-OSS lean the opposite (more compliant). So the concerning behavior does not require frontier capability; it requires a particular kind of safety post-training, and which families have it is an empirical, measurable fact. A single-family study (ours, had we stopped at Qwen) would have wrongly reported a clean negative — the within-Qwen null of §5.3, and the stimulus-driven non-replication, are cautionary illustrations that behavioral safety evals on one model family and one request set are unreliable.

Sharpened thesis. Self-presentation trust at small scale is axis-dependent: trustworthy by incapacity on reasoning-faithfulness and introspection, but eval-conditioned behavior is a trained-in, family-specific property that some 8B models already exhibit in the frontier direction — which is not gated by scale, and is why the paper leads with “family, not scale.” The “trust-by-incapacity” reading survives only as a description of the two cognitive axes.

7. Limitations

  • Sub-frontier scales. Faithfulness is shown on two families (Qwen-1.5B, Llama-3.1-8B), introspection on three (Qwen, Gemma, Llama), eval-awareness on five labs — but all at 1.5B–32B. We cannot reach the frontier scale where introspection / strategic-evaluation phenomena are reported, so positive claims are bounded and negatives are scale-bounded.
  • fp16 generation is the binding cost on an M4. fp16 ≳8B generation is GPU-starved on MPS (≈8B at N=150 did not finish in 2h); the second-family faithfulness run was therefore N=60. Faster hardware (or a quantized white-box path) would lift this.
  • Linear probes / first-token reads. Faithfulness and representation claims are about linear decodability at a specific token; a nonlinear or higher-N probe could find more.
  • Behavioral readouts are coarse and stimulus-sensitive. Refusal classification is regex-based; more importantly, §5.3 shows the result itself can depend on the request set — the MCQA readout missed an effect, a mild refusal set manufactured one, and a harder set dissolved it. Behavioral claims here should be read as holding for the tested stimuli.
  • Single seed / greedy decoding for generation-based results (the faithfulness probe is 4-seed).
  • 4-bit quantization (MLX) for the larger cross-family models. We verified the two that fit (Llama-8B, Gemma-9B) in fp16: the direction is robust but 4-bit inflates magnitudes (Llama +0.24→+0.07), so the 4-bit Δ’s in §5.4 are upper bounds, not point estimates. 14B/32B/20B remain 4-bit-only.
  • Faithfulness causal claim is at token-fraction truncation granularity (not semantic-step ablation) and on one model/task; a step-level or activation-patching counterfactual would sharpen it.
  • CoT (un)faithfulness: Turpin et al. (2023), Language Models Don’t Always Say What They Think; Lanham et al. (2023), Measuring Faithfulness in Chain-of-Thought Reasoning (smaller models more faithful). Closest / concurrent: Cox, Kianersi & Garriga-Alonso (2026), Decoding Answers Before Chain-of-Thought (arXiv:2603.01437) — the same pre-CoT linear probe plus activation steering that flips answers (the causal step we lack), reading the result as “answer predetermined.” We do not claim priority over this concurrent work; our distinct contribution is narrow: the answer-change stratification S={forced≠cot} on which the probe reverses (it falls below chance and emits the pre-reasoning answer), which flips that reading to “load-bearing” on the cases that matter. Adding a Lanham-style causal counterfactual on S is the priority that would let us speak to causality rather than precommitment.
  • Introspection / self-knowledge: Lindsey et al. (2025), Emergent Introspective Awareness in Large Language Models (the protocol we replicate); Binder et al. (2024), Looking Inward; Kadavath et al. (2022), Language Models (Mostly) Know What They Know. Our null is the expected small-scale floor; the contribution is the per-concept artifact dissolution, not the phenomenon.
  • Situational / evaluation awareness: Closest: Abdelnabi & Salem (2025), The Hawthorne Effect in Reasoning Models (arXiv:2505.14617, NeurIPS 2025) — white-box probing/steering shows test-awareness affects safety alignment and “varies in magnitude and direction across models” (reasoning models). We add the un-steered behavioral counterpart across five labs’ standard chat models at 8–20B, with ceiling/floor and over-refusal controls. Also: Anthropic (2025), Claude Sonnet 4.5 System Card (frontier eval awareness); Laine et al. (2024), Situational Awareness Dataset (SAD); Greenblatt et al. (2024), Alignment Faking in LLMs.
  • Refusal / over-refusal: Röttger et al. (2024), XSTest; and false-refusal measurements (e.g. Persona-Prompts, arXiv:2509.08075) used to calibrate Gemma’s ceiling.
  • Activation steering: Turner et al. (2023), Activation Addition; Zou et al. (2023), Representation Engineering.
